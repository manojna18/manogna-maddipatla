Key Features

  • Interactive visualization: I wanted the data to feel alive, not just numbers on a page. Interactive graphics in the story let users explore how different bird populations are changing over time.
  • Illustrations: One of the most fun parts of the project was painting the birds themselves. From the Maui parrotbill to the bluejay and Canadian goose, each illustration was a way to connect with the species and share their beauty with viewers.
  • Scroll-driven experience: I wanted this to feel like a journey rather than a static page. As users scroll, different sections update with new visuals and insights.

Development Process

Planning

I started by creating a loose content flow that outlined how users would move through the story: from the visual introduction to individual bird profiles and their population data. While I didn‚Äôt need complex user flows, I mapped out interaction points where hover-based tooltips would reveal facts about each bird.

Before diving into design, I created simple wireframes to map how the story would unfold visually. I sketched how the user might scroll through sections, with the artwork and the data alternating in focus. To create an immersive experience, I experimented with scrollytelling interactions (using D3.js and Scrollama) to reveal new insights progressively.

The user flow centered on a linear narrative, where each scroll interaction introduces new data points to the user, rather than overwhelming them with data.

My design choices for the story include:

  • Full-width illustrated headers paired with short data highlights
  • Muted natural color palettes (soft blues, greens, ochres) inspired by watercolor field journals and bird guides
  • Subtle typography to keep the data visualizations as the focal point

Challenges

Challenge #1: Finding the right data

Problem: One of the first challenges I faced was working with complex bird survey data. The initial datasets I found were detailed but difficult to use as they included multiple tables, metadata, and scientific codes rather than clear population numbers.

Solution:Eventually, I found a more accessible dataset from the U.S. Geological Survey that summarized long-term bird population trends across the U.S.

Challenge #2: Cleaning the data

Problem: The initial raw data file contained a lot of information I didn‚Äôt need, like species codes, confidence intervals, and scientific names.

Solution: I manually cleaned and simplified the data in Excel, keeping only the columns that were relevant to my story: bird group, year range, and percent change in population. his cleaned version gave me a clear, high-level view of how different bird groups had changed since 1970. I then converted it to JSON format so I could use it with D3.js to build my interactive visualizations.

Implementation

I used D3.js to build custom, data-driven visualizations that could change dynamically as users scrolled through the story. To keep the interaction lightweight, I cleaned the dataset in Excel, exported it to JSON, and structured it so each record represented a bird group and its yearly percentage change in population. This made it easy for D3 to read and render the data directly in the browser. For the scrolling interactions, I used Scrollama.js to trigger animations and highlight different trends as users move through the page. The combination of D3 and Scrollama allowed me to connect data, motion, and narrative in one seamless flow, creating an experience that feels both interactive and informative.
